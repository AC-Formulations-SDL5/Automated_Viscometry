# **Autonomous Rheology Discovery for High-Viscosity Formulation Screening via Physics-Constrained Signal Interpretation**

Mohammad M Rastegardoosta, Ian Ngungab, Koketso Gaborekwec, Frantz Le Devedeca

# Abstract

Rheological characterization of high-viscosity fluids is a recognized bottleneck in formulation science: manual cone-and-plate rheometry requires tens-of-micrometre gap-setting tolerances incompatible with robotic execution. We present an automated viscometry platform that reframes viscosity acquisition as a signal-interpretation problem rather than a geometric-precision one. A cone-shaped rotational torquemeter, Cartesian motion stage, and dual-stage washing module operate under an asynchronous multi-runtime control architecture; the spindle's descent generates torque-displacement signatures decoded by a physics-constrained pipeline calibrated once on Newtonian silicone oils, recovering viscosity at 3.4% mean absolute error with 95.7% of samples within ±10%. The pipeline converts each descent into a calibrated stress-shear-rate curve and, at multiple rotation rates, the fitted power-law equation τ = Kγ̇n. Across six chemistries spanning five orders of magnitude in viscosity (1-125,000 cP), the platform reproduces reference rheology within ±2×, recovers the flow-behaviour index n from 1.00 (Newtonian) to 0.39 (yield-pseudoplastic), and processes 18 samples in ~5 h autonomously using under 20 min (7%) of human time — a 4.7-fold reduction versus manual operation. Both outputs come from one universal pipeline with no per-chemistry retuning, evidence that an information-rich workflow can substitute for the precision hardware of manual rheometry. The platform is positioned as the measurement layer for closed-loop rheology-discovery campaigns, where Bayesian optimization and active learning propose formulations that it characterizes autonomously.

**Keyword:** automated rheology, cone-and-plate viscometry, torque–displacement, physics-constrained inference, power-law fluids, self-driving laboratory, Bayesian optimization, active learning, material discovery, high-throughput characterization.

# 1. Introduction

Precise control of precursor viscosity is an increasingly critical constraint in the development of advanced functional materials. The growing interest in porous, fibrous, and sliding-mode liquid-solid architectures for high-performance triboelectric nanogenerators [[59]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nanoen.2023.108365)[[60]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apmt.2022.101732)[[61]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mtener.2023.101431)[[62]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nanoen.2025.110765)[[63]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mtener.2025.102221) depends on the viscosity of electrospinning dopes, foaming precursors, and aerogel-forming solutions to set the pore and fiber morphology governing dielectric and triboelectric output, yet such formulation viscosities are still characterized manually, with no feedback into synthesis. The bottleneck spans the 1,000–125,000 cP regime underpinning lubricants [[1]](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11249-018-1007-0), specialty coatings [[2]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.porgcoat.2016.07.016)[[3]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.porgcoat.2021.106139), pharmaceutical/injectable formulations [[4]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijpharm.2018.11.012), personal-care emulsions [[5]](https://doi.org/10.1007/s13367-024-00108-y)[[6]](https://doi.org/10.3390/cosmetics12020076), and adhesives [[7]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijadhadh.2012.10.011), where rheology dominates processability and resolving even one composition landscape can demand hundreds of measurements. Manual rotational rheometry is fundamentally limited by operator-controlled gap setting, loading, cleaning, and pacing: metrology guidelines specify gap and zero-point tolerances at the tens-of-micrometre scale [[8]](https://doi.org/10.1515/pac-2013-0601) — precision a trained operator maintains by feel [[9]](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Rheology:+Principles,+Measurements,+and+Applications-p-9780471185758) but an unmodified robotic stage cannot — so characterizing even a modest design-of-experiments campaign routinely costs days of skilled labour, with inter-operator variance degrading comparability.

A common response substitutes experimentation with empirical mixing models — the logarithmic blending rule, Grunberg–Nissan [[10]](https://www.nature.com/articles/164799b0), Ramírez-de-Santiago [[11]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.iecr.4c03240), and Redlich–Kister [[12]](https://doi.org/10.1021/ie50458a036) — interpolating multi-component viscosity from pure-component or binary references. These are accurate in dilute, near-ideal regimes [[13]](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10765-011-1100-1), but break down at industrially relevant concentration extremes [[14]](https://doi.org/10.3389/fenrg.2022.1074699)[[15]](https://doi.org/10.1021/ie060429k): hydrogen-bonding, associative interactions, polymer entanglement, and microgel structuring produce non-ideal behaviour the empirical kernels cannot capture [[16]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fuel.2011.01.017)[[17]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.iecr.2c01487). Blending-rule error for hydrocarbon mixtures rises from near zero to over 13% under colder, more complex conditions [[14]](https://doi.org/10.3389/fenrg.2022.1074699), and in a comparison of seventeen mixing rules for petroleum blends only four remained acceptable, with no single rule generalizing across the full range [[16]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.fuel.2011.01.017). The absence of a reliable computational surrogate keeps physical measurement central to any rigorous formulation programme — it is the cost of measurement, not its elimination, that must be reduced.

Self-driving laboratories [[18]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44160-022-00231-0)[[19]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.chemrev.4c00055) — closed-loop robotic handling, characterization, and ML-guided decision-making — offer a pragmatic solution, compressing development cycles from years to weeks in reaction optimization [[20]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2442-2)[[21]](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aax1566)[[22]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-023-06734-w)[[23]](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aav2211)[[24]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-018-0307-8)[[25]](https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms15733), materials discovery [[26]](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aaz8867)[[27]](https://doi.org/10.1002/adma.202001626)[[28]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.joule.2019.05.014), catalysis [[29]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-020-2242-8)[[30]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.checat.2023.100888), and polymer processing [[31]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-55655-3), including mobile robots that choose their own experiments [[32]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-08173-7). The rate-limiting step is rarely the decision algorithm — active learning already outperforms grid/random search [[33]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-021-03213-y)[[34]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-020-19597-w)[[35]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-023-37139-y)[[36]](https://doi.org/10.1039/C9SC05999G)[[37]](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.abg4930)[[38]](https://doi.org/10.1021/acscentsci.8b00307) — but characterization fast enough to keep pace [[39]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.matt.2021.06.036)[[40]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41578-023-00588-4). Automated viscometry mostly repurposes liquid-handling robots as viscosity proxies: Opentrons (OT-2) platforms calibrated against dispense rate recover Newtonian viscosity over 1,500–12,000 cP at 6.5% error [[55]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D2DD00126H), later widened to 20–20,000 cP at ~8% error with a simple non-Newtonian extension [[56]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D4DD00368C); open-hardware digital pipettes push dispensing accuracy below 1% [[57]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D3DD00115F); and a camera-based inverted-vial method spans 0.01–1,000 Pa·s at ±15–25% error with no contact sensor [[58]](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.02034v1). These, with commercial microfluidic RheoSense-type viscometers, are fast and cheap but return only a single, largely Newtonian viscosity — none recovers a full non-Newtonian constitutive curve autonomously, leaving rotational rheometry underrepresented among self-driving characterization layers.

Closing this gap shifts the burden of precision from hardware to analysis. A densely sampled spindle descent is information-rich: drag versus gap follows a hyperbolic law whose amplitude inverts to viscosity, while its dependence on rotation rate at fixed gap encodes the flow-behaviour index separating Newtonian, shear-thinning [[49]](https://doi.org/10.3390/polym14061262), and yield-stress [[50]](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0377-0257(98)00094-9)[[51]](https://doi.org/10.1007/BF01432034) regimes — both extracted from one descent without relying on absolute geometric accuracy, turning backlash and gap uncertainty into nuisance parameters the analysis absorbs.

In this work we present an end-to-end automated viscometry platform delivering, for any high-viscosity fluid, a calibrated stress–shear-rate flow curve and the fitted power-law equation τ = Kγ̇n. A Cartesian motion stage, cone-shaped rotational torquemeter, and magnetically-driven washing station are integrated into one workstation for autonomous measurement and cleaning across a multi-cell deck. We treat the spindle's descent as a physically structured signal: lubrication theory constrains the drag's functional form, while a data-driven inference layer absorbs the stage's mechanical and geometric uncertainties into nuisance parameters, recovering viscosity and flow-behaviour index without per-chemistry retuning. This one-shot calibration transfers unmodified across six chemistries spanning five orders of magnitude in viscosity (1–125,000 cP), reproducing reference rheology within ±2× and correctly classifying Newtonian, shear-thinning, and yield-stress regimes, while completing 18-sample runs in ~5 h of robot time with under 20 min (7%) of human involvement — positioning the platform as the measurement layer for closed-loop rheology-discovery campaigns driven by Bayesian optimization and active learning [[33]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-021-03213-y)[[35]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-023-37139-y).

# 2. Methods

Every measurement modality in rheology answers a different question about how a material moves, so the choice of modality is itself a design decision with direct consequences for automatability. Rheology spans extensional, oscillatory, and steady-shear deformation, each yielding a distinct descriptor (**Table 1**), but this work targets steady-state shear rheology acquired through a rotational, torque-based measurement for three converging reasons: it is (i) directly automatable on a Cartesian motion stage, (ii) sufficient to recover both the Newtonian apparent viscosity and the power-law exponent that classifies the dominant non-Newtonian regimes encountered in industrial formulations, and (iii) compatible with the dimensional footprint of a multi-cell sample deck. A manual execution of this modality reduces to three operator-controlled steps — loading the sample into a flat-bottom cell, aligning the spindle precisely against the cell floor, and acquiring torque under a programmed angular velocity — and it is exactly these three steps, in that order, that the platform described below replaces with closed-loop, physics-constrained automation.

**Table 1.** Rheology Principles and Methods

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Category | Principle/Method | Key Metric |
| Deformation Type | Shear Rheology | Shear Stress, Shear Rate |
|  | Extensional Rheology | Extensional Viscosity |
| Flow Regime | Steady-state Flow | Apparent Viscosity |
|  | Oscillatory (Dynamic) | Storage (G') & Loss (G'') Moduli |
| Measurement Method | Rotational (Torque-Based) | Torque-Displacement |
|  | Capillary/Pipe Flow | Pressure Drop |
|  | Microfluidics | Shear-thinning profiles |
| Material Response | Newtonian | Linear Stress-Strain relationship |
|  | Non-Newtonian | Power-law index |
|  | Viscoelastic | Phase Angle |

The idea to drive the spindle through a continuous descent, rather than seat it once at a fixed gap, originated directly from manually collected data. Torque was first recorded by hand as a function of spindle height, producing a torque–displacement trace for each material; recasting this torque through the governing viscosity equation into a rotational-drag quantity — the ratio of measured torque to angular speed — turned the raw trace into a drag–displacement signature whose shape varied systematically and distinguishably between materials of different viscosity. Because every parameter of the underlying viscosity equation is encoded in this drag–displacement relationship, the drag amplitude at a given gap correlated directly with viscosity, and it was this correlation, observed first by hand, that motivated treating the full descent — rather than a single measurement at one gap — as the information-bearing signal that the automated platform would later exploit. To establish this relationship, a scientist manually lowered the spindle in discrete increments of 13 μm, spanning increment 33 down to increment −3 relative to the container floor, and at each height tested a series of spindle speeds, recording the resulting torque and back-calculating viscosity from the standard cone-and-plate relation. The full manual procedure required approximately 4.5 hours to characterize a single material and yielded a dataset of 37 rows — one per spindle height tested — and four columns (increment number, spindle speed, measured torque, and measured viscosity).

## 2.1 Hardware Architecture and Automated Workflow

The complete bill of materials is summarized in **Table S1**, and the integrated layout appears in **Figure 1**. Sample containers are stainless-steel, flat-bottom, high-clearance cylinders held in a modular workstation of T-slotted aluminium rails (McMaster) with PETG (FDM-printed) container holders. The rotational sensing element is a 3°-cone-angle, 12.0 mm-diameter stainless-steel spindle coupled to a precision rotational torquemeter (AMETEK Brookfield; full-scale torque Mfull = 7187 dyne·cm) — referred to hereafter simply as the rotational torquemeter, with its manufacturer-quoted flow curves referred to as the reference rheology. Three-axis motion is supplied by a Cartesian CNC stage (Genmitsu 4040-PRO, SainSmart) on NEMA 17 steppers and T10 lead screws, with dedicated peristaltic pumps (Chi.Bio, University of Oxford) metering the washing station's cleaning fluids.

The hardest problem in automating cone-and-plate rheometry is not the measurement itself but cross-contamination: a spindle fouled by the previous sample corrupts every measurement that follows. A dedicated washing station therefore cleans the spindle after every cycle via a bespoke magnetic-repulsion drive — a motor-driven, magnet-embedded spinner in a PETG housing actuates a driven spinner across a 3.0 mm gap inside a separate SLA washing vessel (Rigid 10K Resin V1, Formlabs), so the motor electronics never contact the cleaning fluids — while detergent, water, and isopropanol are sequenced through the pumps. Of four benchmarked protocols of increasing mechanical complexity (static spindle, synchronous co-rotation, lateral oscillation, and a multi-modal rotation + oscillation + brush combination), only the multi-modal protocol left no detectable high-viscosity residue; it was adopted as the platform default and runs concurrently with CNC travel, keeping washing off the critical path.

**Figure 3** summarizes the full workflow: after initialization and per-cell calibration of the safe spindle-to-container height, the platform runs an automated characterization cycle in which the spindle descends incrementally while torque is recorded under a programmed RPM sweep. A physics-constrained feedback layer — combining rotational-drag tracking, statistical trend analysis, confidence-weighted hit-point detection, and second-derivative anomaly detection — identifies the liquid-contact transition and the optimal termination point while protecting the sensor from overload. Each measurement ends with a washing sequence launched concurrently with travel to the next cell, and the resulting torque–displacement record passes to the analysis pipeline — robotic positioning, rheometry, feedback, washing, and data orchestration forming one closed, operator-free loop.

## 2.2 Physics-Informed Rheological Framework

The platform operates as a modified cone-and-plate configuration in which the cone spindle approaches a stationary plate while torque is monitored as a function of vertical displacement. An automated descent, unlike a manual one, inevitably sweeps a far wider gap range than a human operator would ever program, so the torque trace does not stay within a single hydrodynamic regime: it samples parallel-plate-dominated, transition, and cone-and-plate-dominated behaviour in succession, and no single closed-form solution captures all three at once. We therefore adopt a generalized lubrication-theory framework, in the classical tradition of cone-and-plate and parallel-plate rotational rheometry [[9]](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Rheology:+Principles,+Measurements,+and+Applications-p-9780471185758), that interpolates continuously between the two asymptotic limits (**Fig. 4A**).

The local liquid thickness between the rotating cone and the stationary plate is

$$H(r) = h + r\tan\alpha \tag{1}$$

where h is the minimum tip clearance, r is the radial coordinate, and α is the cone half-angle. For large spindle separations (h ≫ r tan(α)), the gap thickness becomes nearly uniform throughout the radius (H(r) = h). In this regime, the system approaches classical parallel-plate rotational rheometry, where the torque scales approximately as:

$$T \propto \frac{\mu\omega R^4}{h} \tag{2}$$

Here, the viscous resistance is strongly governed by the global spindle-to-substrate distance. Conversely, for very small clearances (h→0), the radial contribution dominates the gap profile (H(r) ≈ r tan(α)) and the system converges toward the classical cone-and-plate rheometer limit with nearly uniform shear rate across the radius. The analytical torque expression becomes:

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{3}\frac{\mu\omega R^3}{\tan\alpha} \tag{3}$$

Between these two asymptotic limits lies a transition regime in which the finite tip clearance and the cone geometry jointly set the shear field, and neither the parallel-plate approximation nor the ideal cone-and-plate solution describes it accurately on its own. A generalized lubrication-theory framework was therefore used to capture the evolution of shear stress and torque continuously across the entire displacement range.

Under incompressible, laminar, axisymmetric, low-Reynolds-number conditions, the dominant fluid motion is azimuthal, and radial and axial inertia are neglected, so the governing momentum equation reduces to:

$$\mu\frac{\partial^2 u_\theta}{\partial z^2} = 0 \tag{4}$$

Using no-slip boundary conditions at the rotating cone and stationary plate, the azimuthal velocity distribution becomes:

$$u_\theta(r,z) = \frac{\omega r\, z}{H(r)} \tag{5}$$

which yields the local shear stress:

$$\tau(r) = \mu\frac{\omega r}{H(r)} \tag{6}$$

The total torque was then obtained through radial integration of the local viscous moment contributions:

$$T = 2\pi\mu\omega\int_0^R \frac{r^3}{H(r)}\,dr \tag{7}$$

**Equation 7** provides a unified, physics-informed descriptor that is continuous across the parallel-plate, transition, and cone-and-plate regimes traversed by the automated descent.

Moving from manual to autonomous rheometry does not remove the platform's dependence on gap height — it relocates the burden of controlling it. The Cartesian stage is built around NEMA 17 stepper motors and T10 lead screws with a manufacturer-quoted running accuracy of ±0.01 mm, yet cumulative mechanical uncertainties — backlash, thermal expansion of the metallic guides during continuous operation, the structural compliance of the 3D-printed PETG fixtures, and the static gravitational loading imposed by the ~9 kg torque transducer carried on the vertical axis, which induces cantilever-type flexural deflection of the guide rails and lead screw and thereby superimposes a load-dependent sag on top of the positioning error — can push the effective positional variance to nearly 0.1 mm in the worst case (**Fig.4B**), an order of magnitude beyond the nominal specification. Rather than chase this uncertainty at the hardware level, we exploit the physical structure encoded in equations (1)–(7): because the shape of the torque-versus-gap signature is a strong function of fluid viscosity, a real-time, feedback-driven analysis of the descent can detect both the liquid-contact transition and the safe termination point of the motion without ever needing the absolute value of h. The physics-informed feedback layer therefore (i) absorbs the residual positional uncertainty as a fitted parameter rather than a calibration error, (ii) protects the torque sensor from collision-induced overload, and (iii) shortens the descent duration by terminating each cell as soon as the rotational-drag profile has been sampled densely enough for the inference pipeline.

## 2.3 Data-Analysis Pipeline: from Raw Data to Rheological Behaviour

Every automated-descent record — the triplet (h, T(%), Ω) — passes through exactly one analysis pipeline, regardless of what fluid produced it. **Figure 5** shows the full inference chain, executed identically for Newtonian and non-Newtonian materials with no per-chemistry redesign: the burden of adapting to a new chemistry falls entirely on the data, never on the code.

### 2.3.1 Signal transformation and scaling diagnosis

Before any transformation is applied, each raw descent record is trimmed to discard data acquired after the spindle makes contact with the container floor — an end-of-travel collision that would otherwise inject non-physical torque spikes into the downstream gap–torque regression. Contact is detected in real time by a windowed statistical monitor that evaluates six diagnostic parameters computed over the rotational-drag signal D and its coefficient-of-variation transform CV(D): the coefficient of determination R² of D, of CV(D), and of the first time-derivative D′, together with the second time-derivatives D″, CV(D)″, and (D′)″. Each parameter carries an equal weight of 0.2 in a composite confidence score, so a given frame is flagged as a hit once at least four of the six indicators cross their respective anomaly thresholds simultaneously, i.e., a confidence score ≥ 0.8. To reject isolated noise transients that would otherwise register as false positives, the descent is terminated only after three consecutive hit-flagged frames, at which point the record is truncated to the last pre-contact sample before being passed to the transformation pipeline described below.

The first transformation is rotational-drag normalisation,

$$D \equiv \frac{T(\%)}{\Omega} \tag{8}$$

which removes the trivial linear dependence of torque on angular speed for a Newtonian response and isolates the geometry–fluid coupling that remains. A global and local log-log slope diagnosis on the silicone set immediately ruled out the simplest hypothesis: no single power law D ∝ h−n explains the full descent range (**Fig. 4C**), because the local slope drifts toward −1 only near contact — a pure 1/h model is asymptotically correct but globally incomplete (**Fig. 4D**).

To identify the correct baseline structure, we performed an offset-scan, subtracting candidate constants B from D and refitting the log-log slope on the positive residual (**Fig. 4E**); the resulting plateau near slope −1 confirmed the leading-order form D = A/h + B.

### 2.3.2 Model selection in physical space

Four candidate models — pure hyperbola, regularized hyperbola, generalized power, and saturation — were compared in physical space using bounded Levenberg-Marquardt fitting and ranked by R2, adjusted R2, AIC, BIC, and cross-validated RMSE (**Fig. 4F**). The regularized hyperbola won on every criterion:

$$D(h) = \frac{A}{h + h_c} + B \tag{9}$$

Here, A carries the viscosity information, hc absorbs the effective zero-gap effects (slip layer, asperity, compliance, and residual geometric offsets), and B is a small parasitic baseline.

Residual diagnostics confirm that the selected model is unbiased and near-homoscedastic across gap height, supporting a stable downstream inverse calibration (**Fig. 4G-J**).

### 2.3.3 Universality of geometry shape and fixed-hc production fitting

The factorization hypothesis D(h, μ) = A(μ) F(h) was tested directly: normalizing each sweep by its own maximum and overlaying all silicone traces on a common gap axis. Their collapse onto a single master shape (**Figure S1**) validates a geometry-governed F(h) and a fluid-dependent amplitude A — the central assumption that lets one calibration transfer across every subsequent fluid.

**Figure 4K** confirms that a global fit to this normalized master curve favours the regularized form as the most parsimonious representation, which justifies extracting a single universal geometric regularization length h⋆c for production fitting.

Operationally, each sweep is first fitted with a free hc, and the median of the high-quality fits (R2 > 0.7, hc ∈ [0.05, 1.5] mm) defines h⋆c; every sweep is then refitted with hc fixed at h⋆c, which reduces parameter coupling and improves amplitude identifiability.

### 2.3.4 Calibration equations and constitutive extension

Silicone standards provide the one-shot amplitude–viscosity calibration,

$$\ln A = \ln k + p\ln\mu \iff \mu_\text{app} = \left(\frac{A}{k}\right)^{1/p} \tag{10}$$

For non-Newtonian materials measured at multiple RPM values, the amplitude instead becomes shear-rate dependent,

$$A(\dot{\gamma}) = A_0\,\dot{\gamma}^{n-1} \tag{11}$$

so the slope of “ln A” versus “ln γ̇” returns the flow-behaviour index “n” directly. Shear stress follows from percent torque through the cone-plate conversion

$$\tau\,[\text{Pa}] = c_\tau\,T(\%), \quad c_\tau = \frac{3(M_\text{full}/100)}{2\pi R^3} \approx 1.986\ \text{Pa}/\% \tag{12}$$

Uncertainty and robustness are quantified throughout by leave-the-k-smallest-out sensitivity on near-contact points, 500-iteration bootstrap confidence bands for (A, hc, B), and a distributional analysis of per-sweep hc for universality checking.

## 2.4 Software Orchestration

Rotational sensing, robotic positioning, and washing are integrated within a single control plane. The torquemeter is paired with the three-axis CNC stage for programmable traversal and repeatable spindle alignment, with all machine-level coordinates — sample and washing positions, safe-height offsets, per-cell calibration — parameterized through YAML files so the deck layout can be reconfigured without touching the control software. An ESP32 microcontroller runs the washing subsystem: a custom PCB with PWM-driven L298N motor drivers actuates six pumps and three agitation motors through a multiplexed, diode-isolated channel-sharing configuration, executing detergent/water/isopropanol rinse cycles from a minimal host command interface (**Supplementary Information**).

At the host level, a dual-Python asynchronous architecture accommodates the torquemeter's proprietary 32-bit communication DLL while centralizing orchestration: a 64-bit runtime handles workflow execution, G-code motion control, washing sequencing, and data logging, exchanging data with the isolated 32-bit torquemeter runtime through synchronized file-based communication. Modular hardware-abstraction layers (CNC, viscometer, washing, analysis trigger) enable the non-blocking, concurrent execution of motion, acquisition, and washing that underlies the platform's throughput, alongside traceability (calibration status, software version, environmental conditions) and recovery mechanisms (instrument reconnection, safe-motion interruption). A real-time web dashboard exposes live torque-displacement traces, batch progress, and manual override/abort/calibration controls, closing each batch with an automated report of per-cell results.

**Figure 6** traces this coordination end-to-end: dashboard input/output screenshots (**6A**) and a Sankey diagram (**6B**) show operator actions at the Web Interface flowing through the Python runtimes to the Hardware layer, coordinated by the Workflow Engine into the Data-Analysis Pipeline (Section 2.3) and out to the recovered rheological outputs — so every result remains traceable back to the workflow decision, hardware channel, and operator input that produced it.

# 3. Results and Discussion

The Methods section fixes every equation, fitting rule, and uncertainty calculation used below; what follows is the experimental evidence and its scientific interpretation, presented in two stages: first the acquisition reproducibility and statistical robustness of the automated measurement layer, then the material-level rheological performance across Newtonian and non-Newtonian classes.

## 3.1 Acquisition Performance and Reproducibility

We evaluated the automated measurement layer using silicone oils spanning 1–125 kcP, a Newtonian reference family for calibration and reproducibility testing. Two questions were addressed: can viscosity be recovered accurately, and do the raw drag signatures acquired during spindle descent contain a transferable physical structure that automated inference can exploit?

**Figure 7A** shows representative rotational-drag traces during descent: drag rises monotonically as the gap closes, consistent with lubrication-type flow, and traces remain systematically ordered by viscosity throughout. At this stage of the study, before a per-cell calibration mode existed, the recorded height window was sample-dependent rather than fixed: pooled across the library the stage traversed 1.9 mm (-66.4 to -64.5 mm) in total, but individual sweeps covered as little as ~0.16 mm (120 kcP) to ~0.88 mm (1 kcP). This was not a torque limitation — the 120 kcP oil is still only at 50% of full-scale torque at this gap — but reflected the lack of a standardized per-cell descent window. Per-cell calibration now standardizes the sweep to ~0.4 mm for every cell (≈20 Z-height measurements feeding the rheology pipeline), the protocol used for all subsequently calibrated tests; even so, drag remains far from interchangeable across viscosity, with 1 kcP never exceeding D ≈ 0.56 while 120 kcP never falls below D ≈ 250. **Figure 7B** shows the raw torque–gap data used for model fitting; the highest-viscosity oils read up to 160% of the sensor's rated full-scale torque (100% = 7187.0 dyne·cm) at the smallest gaps, so these near-contact readings carry a priori lower confidence — an instrumentation ceiling revisited for the thermal-interface materials in Section 3.2.3 and flagged as a target for a future hardware revision. Fitting each curve with D(h) = A/(h + hc) + B and normalizing as (D-B)/A tests whether the gap dependence is universal: **Figure 7C** confirms it is, with all samples collapsing from sample-dependent values of 3–7 at zero gap onto a common ~1.5 at a 600 µm gap, isolating viscosity entirely in the amplitude (A) — a factorization that lets one calibration transfer across fluids without chemistry-specific retuning. This calibration holds up under scrutiny: **Figure 7D** shows predicted and reference viscosities tracking the parity line across more than two orders of magnitude, with 22 of 23 samples (95.7%) within ±10% (one outlier, 87.0 kcP, at +13.9%); **Figure 7E** shows no systematic error trend with viscosity; **Figure 7F** shows errors narrowly distributed around zero (mean +0.9%, SD 4.6%, 74% of samples within ±5%); and **Figure 7G** confirms the same agreement sample-by-sample, though absolute error still scales with viscosity magnitude — ~360 cP (2.7%) in the lower half of the range versus ~3,340 cP (4.2%) in the upper half, a roughly nine-fold increase in absolute terms despite the relative error staying single-digit throughout.

We next asked whether the fitted geometric parameter (hc) reflects the measurement geometry rather than being a fitting artefact. **Figure 7H**'s leave-the-k-smallest-out analysis shows (hc) drifting only mildly as near-contact points are discarded — 0.335 mm (full dataset) to 0.422 mm (six points removed), a 26% shift — while its standard error grows from ±0.005 to ±0.024 mm, a graceful loss of precision, with (A) essentially unaffected. Bootstrap resampling agrees: **Figure 7I** shows every point of a representative sample falling inside a narrow 95% reconstruction band, and **Figure 7J**'s bootstrap distribution of (hc) is narrow and unimodal (median 0.23 mm, SD 0.10 mm, 95% interval 0.14–0.50 mm) — small relative to the viscosity span of the set — supporting (hc) as a geometry-linked constant that can be fixed globally, reducing the inverse problem to two free parameters. Uncertainty from the fitting layer is thus well below the material-to-material variation the system is designed to resolve, with mechanical imperfections (backlash, compliance, wetting variability, positioning error) effectively absorbed into the regularized drag representation without compromising viscosity recovery.

**Figure 7K** benchmarks the platform against a human operator on three viscosities (1, 10, and 60 kcP): fitting the same regularized hyperbolic drag model to all nine traces — manual and automated alike — gives R² of 0.982–0.996 in every case, confirming both acquisition modes are governed by the same underlying physical drag law despite differing raw amplitudes from separate gap-zero references. The platform therefore reproduces the same physics a trained operator would extract by hand. **Figure 7L** places this in a throughput context: the full acquisition–washing–analysis–handling sequence processed 18 samples in ~5 hours without operator intervention. Excluding the upstream formulation steps common to both workflows, characterization and washing required 27.0 h of continuous manual operation versus 5.7 h of unattended robot time — a saving of 21.3 h, a ~4.7-fold reduction — without sacrificing data quality, establishing the automated layer as a practical foundation for future closed-loop rheology-discovery campaigns.

## 3.2 Material Performance: Newtonian and Non-Newtonian Materials

Acquisition-level reproducibility answers only part of the question; the platform must also recover physically meaningful rheology across distinct chemistries using one fixed inference workflow. The central test is not whether the system repeats, but whether it preserves constitutive meaning as the material moves from a Newtonian standard to a complex, structure-forming formulation. **Figure 8** assembles the composite evidence.

### 3.2.1 Lubricant (Newtonian Anchor)

Silicone oils serve as the Newtonian anchor and validate the core decoding hypothesis: the drag-amplitude signal maps onto a stable apparent viscosity without geometry-specific retuning. **Figure 8(A-1)** shows the expected single-regime drag response, and the reconstructed stress–shear-rate behaviour stays close to linear — a near-constant μapp over the measured window (**Figure 8(A-2)**). Fitting τ=Kγ̇ⁿ to six representative sweeps returns n=1.00 in every case, the exact Newtonian value, while the consistency index K — numerically equal to the apparent viscosity under the cone geometry — spans 15.4–65.7 Pa·s across the samples. The uniform n=1.00 shows the pipeline classifies Newtonian behaviour unambiguously regardless of viscosity, while the spread in K confirms it simultaneously tracks that viscosity's magnitude — exactly what a chemically simple, weakly structured fluid should return, and the physics-based baseline against which the non-Newtonian results below are anchored.

### 3.2.2 Rheology Modifier Materials

Applying the same pipeline to PEG, Sepineo, Solagum, and Carbopol under multi-RPM sweeps traces a chemically interpretable progression from Newtonian-like to strongly non-Newtonian behaviour. PEG shows mild-to-moderate shear thinning, consistent with chain alignment reducing hydrodynamic resistance at higher shear rates (**Figure 8(B)**): PEG-300K's n rises slightly from 0.82 (5%) to 0.88 (10%) as K rises from 13.9 to 28.8, while PEG-600K's n falls from 1.05 (5%, essentially Newtonian) to 0.74 (6.5%) as K rises from 18.4 to 35.1 — at matched ~5% loading, PEG-600K's higher K (18.4 vs. 13.9) flags its higher viscosity even before either grade departs from Newtonian behaviour. Sepineo and Solagum shear-thin more strongly, reflecting progressive breakdown and reorganization of polymer-entangled and associative networks under shear [[52]](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1359-0294(97)80088-X) (**Figure 8(C-D)**). Sepineo's n drops from 0.69 (1%) to 0.64 (1.5%) before recovering to 0.69 (2%) as K rises from 18.8 to a maximum of 35.9 (1.5%) then falls to 31.1 (2%) — non-monotonic, but confined to a narrow n=0.64–0.69 band, suggesting a network structure not strongly concentration-sensitive over this range. Solagum trends more cleanly: n falls from 0.72 (1%) to 0.71 (2%) to 0.63 (2.5%) as K rises from 16.8 to 27.8 to 30.0, becoming both more shear-thinning and more viscous with concentration. Carbopol approaches yield-pseudoplastic behaviour, with an apparent low-shear resistance that gives way to flow once the material yields [[50]](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0377-0257(98)00094-9) (**Figure 8(E)**): n falls sharply from 0.56 (0.3%) to 0.39 (0.4%), the strongest shear-thinning of any modifier tested, and the 0.3% fit is markedly poorer (R²=0.85) than the other modifier fits (R²≥0.99) — a signature of the low-shear yield plateau that a pure power law cannot fully capture. This progression emerges from a single calibrated model stack: the chemistry-specific behaviour is resolved by the data, not imposed by per-material tuning.

### 3.2.3 Thermal Interface Materials

Thermal interface material (TIM) formulations — SYY, Sekisui B-41, and HY-500 — push the test space toward highly filled, application-driven materials with complex internal architecture. The platform still returns stable drag-to-stress reconstructions within the same autonomous workflow (**Figure 8(F-H)**), but density and fit quality both degrade relative to the lubricants and modifiers. SYY's raw drag profile is visibly non-smooth (**Figure 8(F-1)**), and that noise propagates into the fitted power law τ=21.6γ̇^0.92, which achieves only R²=0.65 (**Figure 8(F-2)**) — far below the modifier fits above — because most readings for this high-viscosity, low-torque-margin sample fall above the sensor's accurate range (Section 3.1). Sekisui B-41 also shear-thins (**Figure 8(G)**), but only three spindle-height conditions stayed within the measurable torque window before lower gaps exceeded it, leaving a fit of τ=41.7γ̇^0.31 at only R²=0.47. HY-500 (**Figure 8(H)**) appears shear-thickening, with a fitted τ=452γ̇^1.81, but that inference rests on only two measured points plus the origin — the nominal R²=1.00 carries no real statistical weight with so few degrees of freedom, so the trend should be read as preliminary rather than definitive. Both limitations trace to the same hardware ceilings — a maximum measurable torque of 7187.0 dyne·cm and a minimum spindle speed of 0.1 rpm — which compress the usable operating window for high-viscosity TIM systems into a handful of points; the TIM results are best read as feasibility-level evidence, a target for the next hardware iteration's torque range and low-speed control rather than a pipeline limitation.

Positioned against the automated-viscometry benchmarks introduced in Section 1, the platform occupies a different point in the accuracy–range–constitutive-detail space. The OT-2 pipetting proxies [[55]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D2DD00126H)[[56]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D4DD00368C) and the camera-based inverted-vial method [[58]](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.02034v1) each return a single apparent viscosity, are limited to Newtonian or weakly non-Newtonian behaviour, and cover at most three decades of viscosity — 20–20,000 cP [[56]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D4DD00368C) or 0.01–1,000 Pa·s [[58]](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.02034v1) — at 6.5–25% error; the digital pipette [[57]](https://doi.org/10.1039/D3DD00115F) is a liquid-handling accessory rather than a viscometer. The present platform spans five orders of magnitude (1–125,000 cP) and recovers a full calibrated stress–shear-rate curve and fitted exponent n per sample, at 3.4% mean absolute error with 95.7% within ±10% (Section 3.1) — accuracy on par with the fastest pipetting proxies despite an order of magnitude more rheological information per measurement. This comes at the expected cost of per-sample speed — a full multi-height, multi-RPM descent-and-wash cycle versus a single dispense stroke — which is why the platform targets batch-level throughput (18 samples in ~5 h) rather than single-datapoint speed. Unlike RheoSense-type commercial microfluidic viscometers, which likewise report a single apparent viscosity per loading, the platform autonomously classifies Newtonian, shear-thinning, and yield-pseudoplastic regimes from one universal calibration, extending automated characterization from proxy Newtonian screening to full non-Newtonian constitutive discovery.

# 4. Conclusion

We have presented an end-to-end automated viscometry platform that converts a single spindle descent into a calibrated stress–shear-rate flow curve and a fitted power-law equation τ = Kγ̇n, via one physics-constrained inference pipeline (Eqs. 1–10) integrating a Cartesian motion stage, cone-shaped rotational torquemeter, and dual-stage washing module. Calibrated once on Newtonian silicone oils (3.4% mean absolute viscosity error, 95.7% within ±10%), the fixed pipeline generalizes with no retuning across six chemistries spanning five orders of magnitude in viscosity (1–125,000 cP), reproducing reference rheology within a ±2× envelope and recovering the flow-behaviour index n from 1.00 (Newtonian) to 0.39 (yield-pseudoplastic Carbopol), while processing 18 samples in ~5 h autonomously — cutting characterization-and-washing time from 27.0 h to 5.7 h (a 4.7-fold, 21.3 h reduction; <7% human involvement). Backlash-limited positioning (~0.1 mm effective variance) and the absence of an operator-tuned gap are absorbed as fitted nuisance parameters (h⋆c, B) rather than engineered away, letting an information-rich analysis layer substitute for the precision hardware of manual rotational rheometry. The platform is intended as the measurement layer for closed-loop rheology-discovery campaigns driven by Bayesian optimization and active learning, with planned extensions to Herschel–Bulkley/Maxwell constitutive models [[51]](https://doi.org/10.1007/BF01432034), inverse molecular-interaction inference, inline NIR/Raman monitoring, and multi-spindle scaling toward a self-driving formulation laboratory.
